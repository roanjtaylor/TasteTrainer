# Taste Synthesis — The Philosophy Behind This App

> Distilled from a 1995 interview with Steve Jobs.
> This is the philosophical spine of the whole application: **develop taste by deliberately
> exposing yourself to the best work humanity has produced, internalize what makes it great,
> and carry those ideas into your own craft.**

---

## 1. The Core Idea — Taste Comes From Exposure to the Best

> "Ultimately it comes down to taste. It comes down to trying to expose yourself to the best
> things that humans have done, and then try to bring those things in to what you're doing."

> "Picasso had a saying — good artists copy, great artists steal — and we have always been
> shameless about stealing great ideas."

> "The way that we're going to ratchet up our species is to take the best and to spread it
> around to everybody, so that everybody grows up with better things and starts to understand
> the subtlety of these better things."

**Takeaway:** Taste is not innate. It is cultivated by repeatedly looking at the best, learning
to perceive its subtlety, and transferring those ideas into your own work.

---

## 2. Taste as a Differentiator — The Microsoft Critique

> "The only problem with Microsoft is they just have no taste. They have absolutely no taste...
> they don't think of original ideas and they don't bring much culture into their product."

> "Proportionally spaced fonts come from typesetting and beautiful books — that's where one
> gets the idea. If it weren't for the Mac they would never have that in their products."

> "Their products have no spirit of enlightenment about them; they are very pedestrian... the
> sad part is that most customers don't have a lot of that spirit either."

**Takeaway:** Taste is not decoration — it is the result of bringing culture and original
thinking from other fields into a product.

---

## 3. Liberal Arts × Technology

> "Part of what made the Macintosh great was that the people working on it were musicians and
> poets and artists and zoologists and historians — who also happened to be the best computer
> scientists in the world."

> "We wanted to pull in the best that we saw in these other fields into this field... I don't
> think you get that if you are very narrow."

> "I view computer science as a liberal art — it should be something that everybody learns."

**Takeaway:** Great work happens at the intersection of disciplines. Breadth feeds taste.

---

## 4. Craftsmanship — Ideas Are Cheap, the Work Is Everything

> "There's a tremendous amount of craftsmanship in between a great idea and a great product...
> as you evolve that great idea it changes and grows; it never comes out like it starts."

> "Designing a product is keeping 5,000 things in your brain... fitting them all together in new
> and different ways to get what you want. It's that process that is the magic."

> The "disease" Scully caught: *thinking that a really great idea is 90% of the work* — when in
> truth the craftsmanship between idea and product is where greatness lives.

> "It's not process, it's content." (Companies decline when they confuse the *process* of
> success with the *content* of it.)

**Takeaway:** The gap between a good idea and a great product is craftsmanship. That gap is the
real work.

---

## 5. A-Players and the Rock Tumbler

> "The dynamic range between average and the best is... 50 to 1, maybe 100 to 1, in software.
> I've built a lot of my success off finding these truly gifted people and not settling for B
> and C players."

> **The rock tumbler:** a team of passionate, talented people "bumping up against each other,
> having arguments, having fights, sometimes making some noise... they polish each other and
> they polish the ideas, and what comes out are these really beautiful stones."

> "What really matters is the work... the most important thing you can do for somebody who's
> really good is to point out when their work isn't good enough — clearly, and articulate why."

> "I don't really care about being right. I just care about success... I don't mind being wrong.
> What matters is that we do the right thing."

**Takeaway:** Surround yourself with the best, hold the work to a high standard, and let the
friction polish the result.

---

## 6. Thinking for Yourself

> "I'd always ask why you do things, and the answers you invariably get are 'that's just the way
> it's done.' Nobody thinks about things very deeply in business... I call it folklore."

> "If you're willing to ask a lot of questions and think about things and work really hard, you
> can learn business pretty fast. It's not rocket science."

**Takeaway:** Question inherited assumptions. Most "rules" are folklore, not truth.

---

## 7. Tools That Amplify Humanity — The "Why" Behind It All

> **The bicycle of the mind:** humans were unimpressive at efficiency of locomotion — until you
> put one on a bicycle, which "blew away the condor." "The personal computer was the bicycle of
> the mind... we build tools that can dramatically amplify our innate human abilities."

> "The best people I've worked with... worked with computers because they are the medium best
> capable of transmitting some feeling you have that you want to share with other people."

> **The vector metaphor:** "We are still really at the beginning of that vector, and if we can
> nudge it in the right directions, it will be a much better thing as it progresses."

**Takeaway:** The point of the tool is to amplify human ability and transmit feeling — nudge the
vector in the right direction early.

---

## 8. The Hippie Ethos

> Asked "hippie or nerd?" — "I'm clearly a hippie." A hippie being someone who senses "there's
> something going on here in life beyond just a job and a family and two cars in the garage...
> the same thing that causes people to want to be poets instead of bankers."

> "That same spirit can be put into products, and people can sense that spirit."

**Takeaway:** Products carry a spirit. People can feel whether the maker cared.

---

## The Throughline

Taste is not innate — it is **cultivated** by repeatedly exposing yourself to the best humanity
has made, learning to perceive the subtlety in it, and then carrying those ideas across domains
into your own craft. That loop — *expose → perceive → internalize → apply* — is exactly what
this taste-trainer is built to create.
